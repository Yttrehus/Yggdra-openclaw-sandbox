## 1. Argument Mapping

**What it is:** Visual representation of reasoning chains — premises, conclusions, objections, rebuttals — using box-and-arrow diagrams. Distinct from concept maps (which show *relational* connections) and mind maps (which show *associational* connections). Argument maps show *inferential/logical* relationships between claims.

**Key distinction from concept mapping:** Concept maps capture cause-and-effect and tangential relationships. Argument maps are restricted to logical support and attack relationships. A concept map asks "how do these ideas relate?" An argument map asks "does this evidence support this conclusion?"

**Key sources:**
- **Stephen Toulmin** (1958) — The Toulmin Model: Claim, Data, Warrant, Backing, Qualifier, Rebuttal. Added the concept of "warrant" (the reason the inference is licensed) which earlier argument diagrams lacked.
- **Tim van Gelder** — Developed Reason!Able, then Rationale (rationaleonline.com). Core research on how argument mapping improves critical thinking.
- **ThinkerAnalytix** (Harvard) — thinkarguments.org, teaching argument mapping in schools.
- **2025 Frontiers review** — "Charting the Field: A Review of Argument Visualization Research for Writing, Learning, and Reasoning" confirms consistent benefits for critical thinking and structured reasoning across disciplines.

**Tools:** [Rationale](https://rationaleonline.com/), [FigJam Argument Map Template](https://www.figma.com/templates/argument-mapping/), CTTSO Argument Mapper.

**2025 development:** Growing integration of LLMs with argument mapping — LLMs maintain the map while students scaffold, contest, and revise reasoning. Argument mapping as a social/collaborative critical thinking activity.

**Brainmap relevance:** HIGH. Our brainmap currently stores *what* Kris knows (facts, notes) but not *why* he believes things (reasoning chains). Argument maps could represent decision rationale — "why did I choose X over Y?" — with explicit evidence links.

**Sources:** [Frontiers 2025 Review](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/education/articles/10.3389/feduc.2025.1709300/abstract), [Tim van Gelder on Argument Mapping](https://timvangelder.com/2009/02/17/what-is-argument-mapping/), [Psychology Today: Argument Mapping & Critical Thinking](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/thoughts-thinking/201811/improving-critical-thinking-through-argument-mapping), [Davies: Concept, Mind, and Argument Mapping](https://www.reasoninglab.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/10/Davies_ConceptMindArgumentmapping.pdf), [Argument Map Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argument_map)

## 2. Wardley Maps (Strategy Mapping)

**What it is:** A visual approach to business/technology strategy created by Simon Wardley (2005, at Fotango). Maps position components along two axes: (1) a value chain (user need at top, dependencies below) and (2) an evolution axis (Genesis -> Custom -> Product -> Commodity). Inspired by Sun Tzu's five factors: Purpose, Landscape, Climate, Doctrine, Leadership.

**Why it matters beyond business:** Wardley Maps solve a problem that SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, and other static frameworks cannot — they show *movement over time*. Components evolve from novel to commodity, and strategy depends on where things are on that curve. This is a living, context-specific mapping technique.

**Core components:**
- Value chain: users -> needs -> capabilities, connected by dependency
- Evolution axis: Genesis (new, uncertain) -> Custom (understood but bespoke) -> Product (standardized) -> Commodity (utility)
- Climatic patterns: predictable forces (e.g., "everything evolves toward commodity")
- Doctrine: universal principles (e.g., "use appropriate methods per evolution stage")

**Key source:** Simon Wardley, "Wardley Mapping, The Knowledge" (book). Free online at [wardleymaps.com](https://www.wardleymaps.com/). Community hub: [github.com/wardley-maps-community](https://github.com/wardley-maps-community/awesome-wardley-maps).

**Brainmap relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Not for daily use, but for strategic decisions about Ydrasil itself: what to build vs. buy vs. use commodity. E.g., mapping "LLM model" (commodity) vs. "personal context layer" (custom) vs. "Kris' judgment" (genesis) reveals where the real value lies. Aligns perfectly with Nate Jones' "scaffolding > models" principle.

**Sources:** [Learn Wardley Mapping](https://learnwardleymapping.com/), [Wardley Maps 101](https://www.wardleymaps.com/guides/wardley-mapping-101), [Wardley Map Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wardley_map), [Dave's Research Primer](https://www.davesresearch.com/wardley-mapping/)

## 3. Systems Thinking & Causal Loop Diagrams

**What it is:** A methodology for understanding complex systems through feedback loops rather than linear cause-and-effect. Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs) are the primary visualization tool — variables connected by arrows showing reinforcing (+) and balancing (-) loops.

**Two fundamental loop types:**
- **Reinforcing loops** — change compounds (growth spirals, vicious cycles). Example: more knowledge -> better decisions -> more confidence -> seek more knowledge.
- **Balancing loops** — change is counteracted (thermostats, homeostasis). Example: more tasks -> more stress -> reduced performance -> fewer tasks taken on.

**Why it matters for knowledge visualization:** Systems thinking moves beyond individual facts to *structural understanding* — the patterns responsible for recurring behavior over time. CLDs make mental models explicit and shareable. This is a higher level of understanding than concept maps, which show static relationships.

**Key sources:**
- Peter Senge, "The Fifth Discipline" (1990) — systems thinking as the cornerstone of learning organizations.
- Donella Meadows, "Thinking in Systems" (2008) — the accessible primer.
- Cascade Institute, "Causal Loop Diagrams: A Short Handbook" (2024) — practical modern guide.

**Brainmap relevance:** HIGH. Kris' work problems (sortering resets, route optimization) and life patterns (burnout, meaning loops) are feedback systems. CLDs could visualize "why does this keep happening?" patterns in a way linear notes cannot.

**Sources:** [Systems Thinker: CLD Basics](https://thesystemsthinker.com/causal-loop-construction-the-basics/), [Sustainability Methods](https://sustainabilitymethods.org/index.php/System_Thinking_&_Causal_Loop_Diagrams), [6Sigma CLD Guide](https://www.6sigma.us/systems-thinking/causal-loop-diagram-in-systems-thinking/), [Cascade Institute Handbook (PDF)](https://cascadeinstitute.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/06/Causal-Loop-Diagrams-Handbook-June-27-2024.pdf)

## 4. Design Thinking Visual Frameworks

**What it is:** A family of collaborative visualization tools used in design thinking processes to organize qualitative data and build shared understanding.

**Key frameworks:**

| Framework | What It Does | How It Works |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Affinity Diagrams** | Cluster large volumes of qualitative data into themes | Individual data points on sticky notes, grouped by similarity. Bottom-up pattern discovery. Used in the "Define" stage. |
| **Empathy Maps** | Externalize what we know about a user type | 4 quadrants: Says, Thinks, Does, Feels. User/persona in the center. Creates shared understanding of user needs. |
| **Journey Maps** | Visualize a user's experience over time | Timeline of touchpoints, emotions, pain points, opportunities. Shows the full arc of an experience. |
| **Stakeholder Maps** | Show relationships between actors in a system | People/groups positioned by influence, interest, proximity. |

**Connection to knowledge organization:** Affinity diagrams are essentially bottom-up categorization — the same principle as card sorting in IA and emergent structure in Zettelkasten. Empathy maps force perspective-taking, which is a form of elaborative encoding (connecting new information to existing schema about people).

**Key sources:** [Nielsen Norman Group: Empathy Mapping](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/empathy-mapping/), [Interaction Design Foundation: Affinity Diagrams](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/affinity-diagrams-learn-how-to-cluster-and-bundle-ideas-and-facts), [Maze: Affinity Diagrams](https://maze.co/blog/affinity-diagrams/).

**Brainmap relevance:** MEDIUM. The *method* (bottom-up clustering) is more relevant than the specific tools. Affinity diagramming could be a way Kris processes voice memos — dump raw thoughts, then cluster them into themes. The empathy map format could work for understanding colleagues, clients, or even "what does future-Kris need?"

## 5. Information Architecture (Card Sorting & Tree Testing)

**What it is:** The discipline of organizing, naming, and structuring content so it is findable and usable. Two core research methods:

- **Card sorting** — participants group items into categories. Reveals *mental models* — how people naturally organize information.
  - Open sort: participants create their own labels
  - Closed sort: predefined categories
  - Hybrid: some predefined, some open
- **Tree testing** — participants navigate a text-only hierarchy to find items. Evaluates whether an existing structure *works*.

**Key distinction:** Card sorting *generates* structure (discovery). Tree testing *validates* structure (evaluation). They're complementary. Card sorting asks "how would you organize this?" Tree testing asks "can you find X in this organization?"

**Why it matters for PKM:** The core insight is that *structure should match mental models*. If Kris organizes knowledge one way but his brain retrieves it another way, the system fails. Card sorting research shows that different people organize the same content differently — so a personal knowledge system should match *Kris'* mental model, not a generic taxonomy.

**Key sources:** [NN/g: Card Sorting Definition](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/card-sorting-definition/), [NN/g: Card Sorting vs Tree Testing](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/card-sorting-tree-testing-differences/), [IxDF: Card Sorting Guide 2026](https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/article/the-pros-and-cons-of-card-sorting-in-ux-research).

**Brainmap relevance:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Before building complex graph structures, we should understand how Kris *actually* thinks about his knowledge. A simple card sort exercise with his existing notes could reveal natural clusters that inform the brainmap's architecture.

## 6. The Feynman Technique & Visual Learning

**What it is:** A learning method attributed to physicist Richard Feynman. Four steps: (1) Choose a concept, (2) Explain it in simple language as if teaching a child, (3) Identify gaps where the explanation breaks down, (4) Review and simplify further. The core principle: if you cannot explain it simply, you do not understand it.

**Why it works (cognitive mechanisms):**
- **Retrieval practice** — actively pulling information from memory strengthens it (testing effect)
- **Generation effect** — producing an explanation is more effective than reading one
- **Elaborative encoding** — connecting the concept to analogies and simple language creates richer memory traces
- **Gap detection** — the simplification process exposes where understanding is shallow

**Connection to visual learning:** The Feynman technique naturally invokes visual aids — diagrams, analogies, metaphors. This aligns with Dual Coding Theory (Paivio): combining verbal and visual representations creates multiple retrieval pathways.

**Key source:** Farnam Street, "The Feynman Technique" (fs.blog) — the definitive modern writeup. Ali Abdaal's adaptation for students. Dennis Learning Center (Ohio State) for academic application.

**Brainmap relevance:** HIGH. The Feynman technique could be a *workflow* in the brainmap — when Kris captures a new concept, the system prompts him to explain it simply (voice memo). The explanation becomes a node that's more retrievable than a raw note because it was generated through active processing.

**Sources:** [Farnam Street: Feynman Technique](https://fs.blog/feynman-technique/), [Farnam Street: Feynman Learning Technique](https://fs.blog/feynman-learning-technique/), [Ali Abdaal: Feynman Technique](https://aliabdaal.com/studying/the-feynman-technique/), [Dennis Learning Center](https://dennislearningcenter.osu.edu/the-feynman-technique/)

## 7. Embodied Cognition — Gesture, Drawing, Physical Manipulation

**What it is:** The theory that cognition is not purely "in the head" but is shaped by the body's interactions with the world. For learning, this means physical actions — gestures, drawing, building — are not just outputs of understanding but *inputs* to it.

**The Drawing Effect (Wammes, Meade, Fernandes, 2016):**
- Participants recalled **more than twice as many** drawn words vs. written words
- The benefit could NOT be explained by elaborative encoding, visual imagery, or picture superiority alone
- Drawing outperformed *all other known mnemonic techniques* including semantic elaboration, visualization, writing, and tracing
- Mechanism: drawing integrates elaborative, pictorial, and motor codes into a context-rich representation
- Works even with simple stick figures — artistic skill is irrelevant

**Gesture in learning (2025 meta-analysis):**
- Technology-based embodied learning (TBEL) shows significant positive effect (SMD = 0.41, p < .01)
- Important caveat: highly embodied tasks can impose excessive cognitive/physical demands, limiting effectiveness
- High-prior-knowledge learners benefit more from embodied instructional methods
- Gesture helps process spatial information and rethink ideas

**Key sources:**
- Fernandes, Wammes, Meade (2018) — "The Surprisingly Powerful Influence of Drawing on Memory" in *Current Directions in Psychological Science*
- Wammes et al. (2016) — "The Drawing Effect: Evidence for Reliable and Robust Memory Benefits in Free Recall" in *Quarterly Journal of Experimental Psychology*
- 2025 Frontiers meta-analysis on technology-based embodied learning

**Brainmap relevance:** HIGH. This is the strongest argument for why a *visual* brainmap matters. It is not just about displaying information — the act of arranging, connecting, and spatially organizing knowledge on a canvas is itself a form of embodied cognition that strengthens memory. A voice-only system misses the motor/spatial encoding channel.

**Sources:** [Fernandes et al. 2018 (SAGE)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0963721418755385), [Wammes et al. 2016 (SAGE)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1080/17470218.2015.1094494), [Frontiers 2025: Embodied Learning Meta-Analysis](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychology/articles/10.3389/fpsyg.2025.1658797/full), [Springer 2024: Research Avenues for Embodied Cognition](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10648-024-09847-4)

## 8. Memory Consolidation During Sleep

**What it is:** The process by which temporary, fragile memories are transformed into stable, long-lasting ones. First noted by Ebbinghaus (1885): less forgetting after sleep than during wake. Modern neuroscience has mapped the mechanisms in detail.

**Key mechanisms:**
- **Hippocampal replay** — during NREM sleep, the hippocampus replays recently encoded experiences, transferring them to neocortical long-term storage
- **Synaptic homeostasis** — sleep downscales synaptic strength globally, preventing saturation while preserving relative differences (strong connections survive, weak ones are pruned)
- **Memory triage** — not all memories are consolidated equally. Emotionally tagged, novel, and goal-relevant memories get preferential consolidation
- **Schema integration** — sleep promotes integration of new memories with existing knowledge structures (schemas)

**Sleep stages and memory types:**
- **NREM (slow-wave sleep)** — declarative memory (facts, events). Most critical for knowledge consolidation.
- **REM sleep** — procedural memory (skills), emotional processing, creative insight
- **Naps (20-30 min)** — effective for refreshing learning capacity, mostly NREM benefit

**Practical implications for learning:**
- Study before sleep, not in the morning (the pre-sleep window is most effective for declarative encoding)
- Space learning across multiple sleep cycles (distributed practice + sleep consolidation = powerful combination)
- Sleep deprivation impairs consolidation — one night of poor sleep can undermine a day of learning

**Key sources:**
- Walker (2017), "Why We Sleep" — the popular science treatment
- Born & Wilhelm (2012), "System consolidation of memory during sleep" — the scientific review
- Stickgold & Walker (2005), "Sleep-Dependent Learning and Memory Consolidation" in *Neuron*

**Brainmap relevance:** MEDIUM. Not directly a visualization method, but deeply relevant to *when and how* knowledge enters the brainmap. If voice memos captured before sleep are consolidated more strongly, the system could flag "pre-sleep captures" as potentially higher-retention. Also relevant: spaced repetition systems should account for sleep cycles.

**Sources:** [Stickgold & Walker 2005 (ScienceDirect)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0896627304005409), [Sleep and Memory Consolidation PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2680680/), [Portland Press: Sleep and Memory Meta-Review](https://portlandpress.com/emergtoplifesci/article/7/5/513/234981/Sleep-and-memory-consolidation-in-healthy), [Memory and Sleep PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7983127/)

## 9. Narrative & Storytelling as Knowledge Organization

**What it is:** The use of narrative structure (characters, setting, conflict, resolution, temporal sequence) as a framework for organizing and retrieving knowledge. Not just a communication tool but a fundamental *memory architecture*.

**Key research findings:**
- **Meta-analysis (33,000+ participants, 75+ samples):** Stories are more easily understood and better recalled than expository essays. The narrative advantage is robust across ages, topics, and contexts.
- **Hippocampal narrative architecture:** The hippocampus forms memories that integrate events into larger, coherent narratives, bridging distant events into connected sequences. Narrative is not just imposed on memory — it reflects how memory is *structured*.
- **Schema scaffolding:** When making memories, brains use schemas as scaffolding — taking prototypical event sequences and filling them with specific details. Narrative structure *is* the schema.

**Why narrative beats lists:**
- Temporal ordering provides built-in retrieval cues (what happened next?)
- Causal connections between events create logical chains
- Emotional engagement tags memories for preferential consolidation (see #8)
- Characters provide perspective anchors (empathy = elaborative encoding)

**Key sources:**
- Bruner (1991), "The Narrative Construction of Reality" — foundational paper arguing narrative is a primary mode of knowing
- Mar et al. (2021), "Memory and comprehension of narrative versus expository texts" — the meta-analysis (Psychonomic Bulletin & Review)
- Cohn-Sheehy et al. (2022), PMC article on schema representations in brain networks supporting narrative memory

**Brainmap relevance:** HIGH. The brainmap should support narrative structure, not just node-and-edge graphs. Kris' voice memos are naturally narrative ("today I drove route X, then Y happened, then I realized Z"). Preserving this narrative structure — rather than atomizing it into disconnected facts — may produce more retrievable memories. The hippocampal research suggests stories are how the brain *wants* to store experiences.

**Sources:** [Springer: Narrative vs Expository Meta-Analysis](https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/s13423-020-01853-1), [PMC: Schema Representations in Narrative Memory](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8993217/), [Stanford CTL: Storytelling as Memory Strategy](https://ctl.stanford.edu/memory-strategy-storytelling), [ScienceDirect: Architecture of Narrative Memory](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212683X16300184)

## 10. AI + Knowledge Graphs + PKM (2025-2026 Frontier)

**What it is:** The convergence of large language models, knowledge graphs, and personal knowledge management tools into integrated systems. This is the most rapidly evolving area and the one most directly relevant to what we are building.

**Key developments:**

| Development | What It Is | Significance |
|------------|-----------|-------------|
| **Cognee** | Cognitive memory layer for AI apps combining graph structures with vector embeddings | Unified memory for AI agents — exactly the architecture we need |
| **Neo4j LLM Knowledge Graph Builder** | Auto-constructs knowledge graphs from unstructured text using LLMs | Eliminates manual graph construction — feed in notes, get a graph |
| **GraphRAG (Microsoft, 2024-25)** | Uses knowledge graphs instead of raw chunks for LLM retrieval. 90% hallucination reduction reported. | Validates graph > flat retrieval for accuracy |
| **LightRAG** | Lightweight GraphRAG with 10x token reduction through dual-level retrieval | Practical for personal-scale systems |
| **Mem.ai** | AI-first PKM that auto-organizes notes, generates tags, discovers connections | Removes organizational friction through automation |
| **Reflect Notes** | Networked notes with end-to-end encryption, manual bidirectional linking | Intentional connection-making with privacy |
| **Heptabase** | Spatial canvas + graph + writing tools | Visual-spatial PKM, closest to "brainmap" concept |

**Key trend:** The field is splitting into two philosophies:
1. **AI-automated** (Mem.ai, Cognee) — the system organizes for you. Optimizes for capture speed and discovery.
2. **Human-intentional** (Reflect, Obsidian) — you organize manually, with AI as assistant. Optimizes for understanding and connection-building.

The research evidence (drawing effect, generation effect, desirable difficulties) suggests that the manual/intentional approach produces *stronger learning*, but the automated approach produces *more complete coverage*. The optimal system likely combines both: auto-capture everything, but prompt the user to manually organize and connect the important pieces.

**2026 industry state:** Knowledge graphs have moved from niche to essential components of intelligent data systems. AI systems with knowledge graph backing outperform by at least 25% (enterprise). Special issue of Semantic Web Journal dedicated to "LLMs, Generative AI and Knowledge Graphs" in progress. IJCKG 2025 conference focused on knowledge graph applications.

**Brainmap relevance:** VERY HIGH. This is the technology stack we are building on. The key architectural decision is the balance between automated and intentional organization — and the research in sections 6, 7, and 9 above informs that balance.

**Sources:** [Beam.ai: 5 Ways KGs Reshape AI 2025-2026](https://beam.ai/agentic-insights/5-ways-knowledge-graphs-are-quietly-reshaping-ai-workflows-in-2025-2026), [Claudiu Branzan: LLMs to Knowledge Graphs 2025](https://medium.com/@claudiubranzan/from-llms-to-knowledge-graphs-building-production-ready-graph-systems-in-2025-2b4aff1ec99a), [Semantic Web Journal Special Issue](https://www.semantic-web-journal.org/blog/special-issue-large-language-models-generative-ai-and-knowledge-graphs), [Glukhov: PKM Goals & Tools 2025](https://www.glukhov.org/post/2025/07/personal-knowledge-management/), [AI Graph-Based PKM (Medium)](https://medium.com/@theo-james/ai-graph-based-personal-knowledge-management-c0e09ac55654)

## Cross-Cutting Synthesis: What Pass 2 Adds to Pass 1

### New Visualization Types (Pass 1 had: mind maps, concept maps, knowledge graphs)
- **Argument maps** — for reasoning chains and decision rationale
- **Wardley maps** — for strategic positioning and evolution over time
- **Causal loop diagrams** — for feedback systems and recurring patterns
- **Affinity diagrams** — for bottom-up clustering of qualitative data
- **Empathy maps** — for perspective-taking and user understanding
- **Journey maps** — for temporal experience visualization

### New Cognitive Foundations (Pass 1 had: dual coding, chunking, schemas, elaborative encoding)
- **The drawing effect** — physically creating visuals produces 2x retention vs. writing, outperforms all known mnemonics
- **Embodied cognition** — the body is part of cognition, not just output. Gesture and spatial manipulation strengthen learning.
- **Sleep consolidation** — memory is not just encoded, it is consolidated during sleep. Timing of learning matters.
- **Narrative memory** — stories are not just a communication format but a fundamental memory architecture. The hippocampus organizes memories narratively.
- **Feynman technique** — simplification as understanding test, combining retrieval practice + generation effect + gap detection

### New Design Principles for the Brainmap
1. **Support multiple visualization types** — not just nodes-and-edges. Some knowledge is hierarchical (mind map), some is logical (argument map), some is systemic (causal loops), some is temporal (narrative/journey).
2. **Include manual interaction** — the drawing effect and embodied cognition research show that passive display is less effective than active manipulation. The brainmap should invite dragging, connecting, arranging.
3. **Preserve narrative** — voice memos and session logs should maintain their story structure, not be atomized into disconnected facts.
4. **Account for timing** — pre-sleep captures may consolidate better. Spaced repetition should respect sleep cycles.
5. **Balance automation and intention** — auto-capture everything, but prompt manual organization of important pieces. The act of organizing is itself a learning event.
6. **Match Kris' mental model** — before imposing structure, discover how Kris naturally categorizes his knowledge (card sorting principle).
